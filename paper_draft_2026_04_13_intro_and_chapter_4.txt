Abstract
 Key points:
1. 1. Introduction
 Metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) is the most prevalent chronic liver disease globally, affecting approximately 30–40% of adults and constituting a major component of the worldwide metabolic disease landscape [1]. While traditionally viewed as a liver-restricted condition characterized by excessive hepatic lipid accumulation, increasing evidence indicates that MASLD is a systemic disease that contributes to dysfunction across multiple organs. Beyond progressive liver pathology - including steatohepatitis, fibrosis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma - MASLD is strongly associated with a wide range of extrahepatic manifestations such as cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, neurodegenerative disorders, and type 2 diabetes [2]. These systemic manifestations make the clinical course of MASLD highly heterogeneous. Patients differ substantially in disease progression, severity of hepatic injury, and risk of developing extrahepatic comorbidities. While some individuals remain in relatively stable steatosis states for decades, others progress rapidly toward advanced liver disease and systemic metabolic complications.
 Given this biological and clinical heterogeneity, there is an increasing need to move beyond a uniform diagnostic label toward phenotype-based characterization of MASLD. Phenotyping aims to identify subgroups of patients with distinct metabolic, molecular, and clinical profiles that correspond to different disease mechanisms and risk trajectories. Such stratification is essential for improving prediction of disease progression and extrahepatic comorbidity development, enabling earlier intervention and more personalized monitoring strategies.
 Recent advances in data science and machine learning provide powerful tools for uncovering latent patterns within complex clinical datasets, enabling data-driven discovery of MASLD phenotypes. By integrating laboratory biomarkers, imaging data, and longitudinal clinical outcomes, these approaches can help identify patient subgroups with shared biological characteristics and differential risk of multi-organ complications. Understanding these phenotypic structures may support precision medicine approaches in MASLD, improving risk stratification and guiding targeted prevention strategies aimed at preserving systemic metabolic health and healthspan.

 The preceding sections have demonstrated that MASLD is not merely a liver-specific disorder but a systemic metabolic disease that drives dysfunction across multiple organ systems - including the cardiovascular system, kidneys, brain and pancreas. The heterogeneity of these extrahepatic manifestations, combined with the wide interindividual variability in disease progression, underscores a fundamental clinical challenge: patients carrying the same MASLD diagnosis may follow markedly different trajectories of multi-organ deterioration. Addressing this challenge requires systematic, data-driven approaches capable of uncovering latent patient subgroups and stratifying risk for specific comorbidities. The following sections formulate this problem in terms of machine learning paradigms and present a structured analytical framework for MASLD phenotyping and risk stratification.
4. Machine learning framework for MASLD phenotyping and risk stratification
4.1. Problem formulation
One of the main goals of this work is to address the clinical challenge of stratifying patients with an established diagnosis of MASLD according to their underlying biological heterogeneity and their differential propensity for extrahepatic disease development over time. As a chronic metabolic disorder, its progression and associated systemic manifestations can significantly impair healthspan. At the same time, considerable interindividual variability in disease expression and progression is observed among patients with MASLD, underscoring the need for systematic phenotypic characterization that can refine risk stratification and delineate patient profiles with distinct disease trajectories. Such an approach may improve the prediction of long-term comorbidity burden and support more personalized monitoring and preventive strategies.
 In recent years, efforts have been made to characterize MASLD phenotypes using data-driven methods. A study by Gina Ryu et al., using RNA sequencing and non-negative matrix factorization, identified three distinct MASLD clusters: a with heterogeneous clinical and molecular profiles. Cluster I was characterized by lean patients with MASLD and relatively mild metabolic dysfunction. In contrast, Cluster III had high rates of hypertension and diabetes, along with advanced intrahepatic fibrosis. Cluster II represented obese patients with MASLD and a high prevalence of metabolic dysfunction [1]. In another study, Violeta Raverdy et al. applied a data-driven clustering approach to clinical data and identified two MASLD subtypes: a liver-specific subtype associated with more rapid liver disease progression, and a cardiometabolic subtype characterized by hyperglycemia and dyslipidemia, with increased risks of cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes [2]. Another study by Chang Hong et al., using K-means clustering, identified five MASLD subtypes: metabolic-dyslipidemia, younger, obesity, inflammatory, and hepatotoxic. Metabolic outcomes differed across these subtypes; the hepatotoxic subtype showed a markedly higher risk of severe liver disease compared with the metabolic-dyslipidemia subtype, while the inflammatory subtype had the highest burden of extrahepatic complications. Accordingly, the hepatotoxic and inflammatory subtypes were classified as high-risk, and the remaining three as low-risk  [3]. Most recently, Huneault et al. applied unsupervised clustering to pediatric MASLD data and identified three clinically distinct metabotypes: early-mild, cardiometabolic, and inflammatory-fibrotic [4].

4.2. Preparing MASLD clinical data for machine learning
 Real-world MASLD clinical data present several preprocessing challenges that must be addressed before applying any ML workflow. Missing data are common and non-random: specialized measurements such as liver stiffness or laboratory data such as insulin resistance markers (HbA1c, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, C-peptide) or lipid metabolism markers (HDL, LDL, total cholesterol, triglycerides) are frequently absent for patients without metabolic risk flags, introducing informative missingness that simple mean imputation would distort; multiple imputation or missingness indicator encoding is preferable. High collinearity is inherent to MASLD biomarker panels - liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT) co-vary strongly, as do components of the lipid panel and insulin resistance proxies - making feature selection essential both to prevent model instability and to preserve clinical interpretability. Feature selection can be performed using model-based regularization (e.g., LASSO), wrapper methods (e.g., Boruta), or post-hoc importance analysis (e.g., SHAP). Finally, continuous biomarkers such as BMI, GFR, and transaminases typically require normalization or log-transformation to reduce the influence of extreme values that are clinically meaningful but statistically disruptive.
4.3. Machine learning paradigms and analytical scenarios for phenotypic characterization of MASLD
The field of machine learning is conventionally organized into three fundamental paradigms: supervised learning, unsupervised learning, and reinforcement learning. Although reinforcement learning holds promise for personalized treatment optimization, it requires dense sequential per-patient data that are rarely available in current MASLD cohorts and is therefore beyond the scope of this review. Semi-supervised learning, a hybrid paradigm that extends the supervised objective by exploiting unlabeled data through techniques such as self-training (pseudo-labeling) or graph-based label propagation, is similarly relevant to MASLD cohorts where outcome labels are only partially observed but is likewise beyond the scope of this review.
Thus, the design of a machine learning workflow for MASLD phenotyping is shaped by two fundamental considerations:
1. the learning paradigm - whether labeled clinical outcomes are available to guide model training (supervised learning) or whether the goal is hypothesis-free discovery of latent patient structure (unsupervised learning);
2. the data structure - whether longitudinal follow-up with time-stamped events is recorded, giving rise to censored time-to-event data, or whether outcomes are ascertained within a fixed observation window and represented as binary or categorical labels (simplified data).
Crossing these two dimensions yields four analytical scenarios (Scenarios 1–4, Table 6.2), each with distinct methodological requirements, applicable models, and validation strategies: Scenario 1 - supervised learning on censored data (survival modeling); Scenario 2 - supervised learning on simplified data (fixed-horizon classification); Scenario 3 - unsupervised clustering with survival-based validation; Scenario 4 - unsupervised clustering with outcome-rate validation (see chapter6_matrix.mermaid for the source). The following subsections walk through each scenario in turn: §4.3.1 covers Scenarios 1 and 2 (the two supervised settings) and §4.3.2 covers Scenarios 3 and 4 (the two unsupervised settings).

4.3.1. Supervised Learning: predicting extrahepatic comorbidity development
 Supervised learning maps input features to known outcome labels. In MASLD, input features span clinically meaningful biomarker domains — lipid panels (HDL, LDL, total cholesterol, triglycerides), insulin resistance markers (HbA1c, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, C-peptide), liver function tests (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, bilirubin, albumin, total protein), liver stiffness, inflammatory markers (IL-6, TNF-α), kidney function (urea, creatinine, GFR, urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio), thyroid markers (TSH, fT3, fT4), and anthropometrics (BMI) — while outcome labels represent endpoints such as cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, CKD, neurodegeneration, or advanced fibrosis. Since MASLD patients frequently develop multiple comorbidities simultaneously or sequentially, the problem is naturally formulated as multi-label classification or, alternatively, as multi-task learning, where a shared architecture jointly learns related predictions through shared metabolic representations.
Scenario 1 - Supervised learning with censored data: survival modeling.
 When longitudinal follow-up with time-stamped events is available, the framework extends to survival analysis, which handles censored observations and models when, not merely whether, an outcome occurs - a distinction critical for clinical decision-making, as comorbidity timing directly influences monitoring intervals and therapeutic urgency.
Applicable methods:
* Cox proportional hazards model: classical, interpretable; estimates covariate effects on baseline hazard. Assumes proportional hazards and linear effects.
* Random survival forests (RSF): non-parametric; captures non-linear interactions and handles missing data natively.
* Gradient-boosted survival models (XGBoost-Cox, LightGBM-Survival): combine gradient boosting flexibility with Cox partial likelihood loss for high-dimensional feature spaces.
* DeepSurv: neural network Cox extension capturing non-linear covariate effects; requires large cohorts.
* DeepHit: deep learning for competing risks; models joint event-time distributions without the proportional hazards assumption — relevant when one comorbidity alters the risk of another.
* Fine-Gray model: models subdistribution hazard for competing risks, enabling cumulative incidence estimation.
Validation: C-index, time-dependent AUC, Brier score, decision curve analysis (DCA).
Interpretation: SHAP values, permutation importance, partial dependence plots.
Strengths: Handles censoring; models event timing; supports competing-risk formulations essential for multi-comorbidity settings; compatible with a Markov state-transition perspective where transitions from MASLD to comorbid states are governed by cause-specific hazards.
Limitations: Requires time-stamped events, which may be unavailable in some registries; Cox assumes proportional hazards; deep learning approaches need larger cohorts and post-hoc explanation.
Scenario 2 - Supervised learning with simplified data: fixed-horizon classification.
 When precise event times are unavailable or the clinical question targets a fixed horizon (e.g., "will this patient develop CVD within 2 years?"), outcomes are reduced to binary or categorical labels within a defined time window.
Applicable methods:
* Logistic regression: interpretable baseline; penalized variants (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net) address biomarker collinearity and perform embedded feature selection.
* Support vector machines (SVM): effective for moderate-dimensional data; kernel variants capture non-linear decision boundaries.
* Random Forest: captures non-linear interactions, handles mixed data types, provides built-in feature importance.
* Gradient boosting machines (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost): state-of-the-art for tabular clinical data; handle missing values natively and offer SHAP-based interpretability. In MASLD, these models have outperformed conventional scoring systems such as FIB-4 and the NAFLD Fibrosis Score for advanced fibrosis screening [1].
* Feedforward neural networks: extend to high-dimensional spaces integrating clinical, genomic, and imaging data; require larger datasets and post-hoc explanation.
Validation: AUROC, AUPRC, F1 score, calibration curves, DCA.
Interpretation: SHAP values decompose each prediction into per-feature contributions.
Strengths: Simpler formulation; clinically familiar; works with smaller datasets; no time-stamped events required.
Limitations: Discards event timing; patients with follow-up shorter than the prediction window must be excluded or treated as negative (survivorship bias); window choice is arbitrary.
4.3.2. Unsupervised learning for phenotype discovery
 Unsupervised learning discovers inherent structure in data without outcome labels. For MASLD, this paradigm is especially valuable for hypothesis-free phenotype discovery - identifying patient subgroups with shared biomarker profiles that may correspond to distinct disease mechanisms and risk trajectories not anticipated by prior clinical knowledge.  Dimensionality reduction is typically applied before clustering. PCA identifies linear variance axes (often corresponding to metabolic syndrome severity, hepatic injury, or renal decline). NMF decomposes data into additive, non-negative components that are more biologically interpretable — e.g., isolating metabolic decline, inflammatory activation, or fibrotic remodeling — and has been applied to MASLD transcriptomics [1, §4.1]. UMAP and t-SNE project data into 2D/3D for visual assessment of cluster separation. Autoencoders capture non-linear latent structure that linear methods may miss.
 Clustering algorithms include: K-means (most used in MASLD phenotyping [3, §4.1]; assumes spherical, equal-sized clusters); Gaussian mixture models (probabilistic assignments, accommodates overlapping phenotypes); hierarchical clustering (dendrograms allow post-hoc granularity selection); HDBSCAN (no pre-specified K, identifies outliers); and spectral clustering (detects non-convex structures via similarity graphs). Optimal K is determined through silhouette score, Davies–Bouldin index, and gap statistic, complemented by bootstrap stability analysis.
 Once clusters are identified, they are characterized post hoc by examining dominant biomarker domains at each centroid and validated against clinical outcomes (cardiovascular events, diabetes incidence, liver-related mortality) to confirm phenotypic relevance. The two unsupervised scenarios in our framework share the same clustering step but differ in the post-hoc validation strategy.
Scenario 3 - Unsupervised clustering with survival-based validation.
 When time-to-event data are available, cluster relevance is assessed strictly post hoc (outcome data are not used during clustering) via Kaplan–Meier curves with log-rank tests, multivariable Cox regression adjusting for confounders (age, sex, BMI), and competing-risks analysis.
Strengths: Rigorous temporal evidence of distinct risk trajectories; appropriate censoring handling.
Limitations: Requires longitudinal follow-up; observational — causal interpretation requires caution.
Scenario 4 - Unsupervised clustering with outcome-rate validation.
 When only binary or categorical outcomes are available, validation compares prevalence across clusters via chi-square or Fisher's exact tests, confounder-adjusted logistic regression, and outcome-distribution heatmaps.
Strengths: Works without event-time data; applicable to cross-sectional or limited-temporal-resolution registries.
Limitations: Less informative than survival-based validation; loses temporal granularity.
4.4. An integrated workflow for MASLD phenotyping
Having surveyed the supervised and unsupervised paradigms, neither alone can satisfy the problems set out in §4.1., discovering MASLD phenotypes and quantifying their differential comorbidity risk, because in clinical practice these are inseparable steps of the same decision process: meaningful risk stratification presupposes knowing which patient subtype one is dealing with. An integrated workflow is therefore required that draws on all four scenarios of Table 6.2, which first calls for understanding how their roles differ and complement each other.

	 As the table makes clear, the supervised and unsupervised approaches are complementary rather than competing. The supervised pathway directly optimizes for outcome prediction and yields individual-level risk scores with per-feature explanations, making it immediately actionable in clinical decision-making. The unsupervised pathway discovers latent patient structure without requiring outcome labels, potentially revealing biologically meaningful subtypes that no single outcome variable would capture. This complementarity motivates a sequential workflow in which unsupervised discovery precedes supervised prediction.
 The resulting workflow is organized into five phases (Figure 5). Starting from biomarker feature vectors assembled during data preparation (Phase 1), unsupervised clustering is applied to discover candidate phenotypes without influence from outcome data (Phase 2). These candidate phenotypes are then validated against held-out clinical outcomes — using survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier curves, Cox regression) when time-to-event data are available (Scenario 3), or outcome-rate comparisons (chi-square tests, logistic regression) when only binary endpoints are recorded (Scenario 4) (Phase 3). Once phenotypes are confirmed to differ significantly in their clinical trajectories, phenotype-specific supervised models — whether survival models (Cox, RSF, DeepHit; Scenario 1) or fixed-horizon classifiers (XGBoost, logistic regression; Scenario 2) — are trained within each subgroup to generate individualized, per-comorbidity risk scores (Phase 4). At deployment, a new patient's biomarker vector is first assigned to the most likely phenotype via the clustering model, and the corresponding supervised model then produces a personalized risk profile that informs monitoring frequency, preventive interventions, and therapeutic decisions (Phase 5). This combined workflow leverages the discovery feature of unsupervised learning with the predictive precision of supervised modeling and forms the basis for the clinical translation discussed in the following section.

4.5. From phenotype to clinical action
The ultimate purpose of MASLD phenotyping is not cluster assignment per se, but its translation into differentiated clinical management. The phenotypic archetypes identified in §4.1 converge on a consistent landscape across independent cohorts and methods: a hepatotoxic or fibrosis-dominant subtype [1, §4.1; 3, §4.1], a cardiometabolic subtype [2, §4.1; 3, §4.1], an inflammatory subtype with high extrahepatic burden [3, §4.1; 4, §4.1], and a metabolically mild subtype [1, §4.1; 4, §4.1]. This convergence provides an empirical basis for translating phenotype assignments into care pathways, and once a patient is assigned to a phenotype, that assignment should carry concrete clinical implications.
 Hepatotoxic or fibrosis-dominant phenotype. Corresponding to Ryu et al.'s Cluster III and Hong et al.'s hepatotoxic subtype — both defined by elevated transaminases, high liver stiffness, and advanced fibrosis markers — this group carries the greatest risk of liver-related endpoints [1, §4.1; 3, §4.1]. Management warrants intensified hepatological surveillance with shortened FibroScan intervals, early consideration of antifibrotic therapy such as resmetirom, and hepatocellular carcinoma screening protocols calibrated to fibrosis stage. In the supervised learning flow (Figure 5, Phase 4), models for this phenotype should prioritize hepatic feature domains — liver stiffness, transaminases, and fibrosis scores — as the primary predictive signals.
 Cardiometabolic phenotype. Corresponding to Raverdy et al.'s cardiometabolic subtype and Hong et al.'s metabolic-dyslipidemia cluster — characterized by dyslipidemia, insulin resistance, and elevated cardiovascular risk scores — this group's dominant hazard is cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes rather than advanced liver pathology [2, §4.1; 3, §4.1]. Management shifts toward aggressive lipid control, GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy, and cardiology co-management, with monitoring intervals calibrated to cardiovascular event risk. Supervised models for this phenotype should weight lipid panel components, insulin resistance markers, and blood pressure trajectories accordingly.
 Inflammatory phenotype. Corresponding to Hong et al.'s inflammatory subtype and Huneault et al.'s inflammatory-fibrotic metabotype — defined by elevated IL-6, TNF-α, and concurrent multi-organ comorbidity burden — this group carries the highest extrahepatic complication load across published studies [3, §4.1; 4, §4.1]. Management warrants lower thresholds for specialist referral across nephrology, cardiology, endocrinology, and neurology, with particular attention to chronic kidney disease progression and cognitive decline, given the convergent metabolic-inflammatory mechanisms linking hepatic, renal, and neurological function.
 Lean or metabolically mild phenotype. Corresponding to Ryu et al.'s Cluster I and Huneault et al.'s early-mild metabotype, this group presents with the lowest short-term complication burden [1, §4.1; 4, §4.1]. However, metabolically mild status does not imply a stable trajectory. As outlined in the introduction, age-related processes — cellular senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction, impaired autophagy, and oxidative stress — progressively erode hepatic metabolic reserve even in the absence of overt metabolic syndrome. Patients assigned to this phenotype therefore require periodic reassessment, with re-clustering at defined intervals to detect trajectory shifts before clinical complications emerge; the workflow described in §4.4 supports this by treating phenotype assignment as a dynamic, revisable classification rather than a fixed label.
In this way, the ML framework described in the preceding sections does not replace clinical judgement but operationalises it — converting heterogeneous patient profiles into structured, phenotype-specific care pathways. Phase 5 of the workflow (see Figure 5) is precisely this translation step: phenotype assignment gates the selection of a comorbidity-specific supervised model, and the resulting individualized risk profile directly informs monitoring frequency, preventive interventions, and referral decisions across the systemic manifestations MASLD drives.

 Conclusion
 MASLD, a systemic disorder closely linked to aging, has extensive extrahepatic manifestations, including CVD, CKD, neurodegeneration, and T2DM. It requires data-driven phenotyping due to its marked clinical and biological heterogeneity in progression and multi-organ risk. Advanced clustering can identify hidden patient subgroups with distinct comorbidity patterns and disease trajectories, enabling precise risk stratification. Therapeutically, this stratification facilitates precision medicine through personalized follow-up protocols, such as intensified surveillance for high-risk clusters susceptible to fibrosis or extrahepatic events. This approach enhances prognostic accuracy, optimizes interventions, and enables tailored prevention strategies to extend healthspan.
