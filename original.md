Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease as a systemic aging disorder: extrahepatic manifestations and the need for data-driven phenotyping



Abstract
 Metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) is the most prevalent chronic liver disease worldwide, affecting approximately 30–40% of the adult population. Although traditionally viewed as a liver-specific disorder, MASLD is increasingly recognized as a systemic metabolic disease associated with multiple extrahepatic complications, including cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, neurodegenerative disorders, and type 2 diabetes. Aging of the liver represents an important biological factor contributing to the development and progression of MASLD, as age-related processes such as cellular senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and impaired autophagy disrupt hepatic metabolic homeostasis and promote lipid accumulation and inflammation. Despite its high prevalence, MASLD exhibits substantial clinical and biological heterogeneity, with patients demonstrating markedly different disease trajectories and risks of multi-organ dysfunction. Recent advances in machine learning provide powerful tools for identifying latent phenotypes within complex clinical and biomarker datasets. Such an approach may improve the prediction of long-term comorbidity burden and support more personalized monitoring and preventive strategies. This review summarizes current knowledge on MASLD as a multi-organ disease and discusses how machine learning–based phenotyping can improve risk stratification, uncover biological heterogeneity, and support precision medicine strategies for MASLD management and prevention.

1. Introduction

Metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) is the most prevalent chronic liver disease globally, affecting approximately 30–40% of adults and constituting a major component of the worldwide metabolic disease landscape [1]. While traditionally viewed as a liver-restricted condition characterized by excessive hepatic lipid accumulation, increasing evidence indicates that MASLD is a systemic disease that contributes to dysfunction across multiple organs. Beyond progressive liver pathology - including steatohepatitis, fibrosis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma - MASLD is strongly associated with a wide range of extrahepatic complications such as cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, neurodegenerative disorders, and type 2 diabetes [2]. These systemic manifestations arise from complex metabolic, inflammatory, and endocrine interactions linking the liver to other organs.

The systemic nature of MASLD highlights the liver’s role as a central regulator of metabolic homeostasis and suggests that hepatic dysfunction can accelerate organism-wide metabolic deterioration and aging processes. However, the clinical course of MASLD is highly heterogeneous. Patients differ substantially in disease progression, severity of hepatic injury, and risk of developing extrahepatic comorbidities. While some individuals remain in relatively stable steatosis states for decades, others progress rapidly toward advanced liver disease and systemic metabolic complications. This variability reflects the interplay of multiple determinants, including genetic predisposition, metabolic status, environmental exposures, and age-related changes in hepatic physiology.

Given this biological and clinical heterogeneity, there is an increasing need to move beyond a uniform diagnostic label toward phenotype-based characterization of MASLD. Phenotyping aims to identify subgroups of patients with distinct metabolic, molecular, and clinical profiles that correspond to different disease mechanisms and risk trajectories. Such stratification is essential for improving prediction of disease progression and extrahepatic comorbidity development, enabling earlier intervention and more personalized monitoring strategies.

Recent advances in data science and machine learning provide powerful tools for uncovering latent patterns within complex clinical datasets, enabling data-driven discovery of MASLD phenotypes. By integrating laboratory biomarkers, imaging data, and longitudinal clinical outcomes, these approaches can help identify patient subgroups with shared biological characteristics and differential risk of multi-organ complications. Understanding these phenotypic structures may support precision medicine approaches in MASLD, improving risk stratification and guiding targeted prevention strategies aimed at preserving systemic metabolic health and healthspan.

2. Physiological aging of the liver

The liver plays a vital role in maintaining metabolic homeostasis. Its remarkable regenerative capacity has led to the belief that it is less impacted by aging compared to other organs. However, chronic liver diseases, notably metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), increase in prevalence with age, affecting approximately 38% of all adults and between 7% and 14% of children and adolescents worldwide [1].
MASLD can elevate the risk of developing metabolic disorders including type 2 diabetes, as well as cardiometabolic, renal, and neurodegenerative diseases, and may advance to severe inflammatory liver conditions like cirrhosis and liver cancer. Despite its high incidence, the molecular mechanisms driving age-related liver diseases remain poorly understood. Aging impairs liver function through remodeling and loss of distinct hepatic zonation, cellular senescence, impaired hepatic autophagy, mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and altered metabolic signaling, which contribute to the development and progression of MASLD by disrupting lipid and glucose metabolism, promoting inflammation, and reducing the liver’s regenerative capacity  [2],[6],[10],[14].

2.1. Hepatic zonation

Hepatic zonation refers to the structured arrangement of hepatocytes within the liver lobule into distinct zones, each specializing in specific metabolic tasks. This spatial organization enables the liver to manage a diverse array of complex metabolic processes efficiently by allocating distinct functions to different populations of hepatocytes. The liver lobule is generally divided into three zones along the portocentral axis: Zone 1 (periportal), Zone 2 (mid-lobular), and Zone 3 (perivenous). Zone 1 hepatocytes, located near oxygen-rich blood sources, specialize in metabolic activities such as gluconeogenesis, β-oxidation of fatty acids, amino acid metabolism, and urea synthesis. In contrast, Zone 3 hepatocytes, situated near the central vein where oxygen levels are lower, are primarily responsible for lipid synthesis, glycolysis, drug detoxification, and glutamine production. This compartmentalization maintains metabolic balance, optimizes energy utilization, and allows the liver to adapt quickly to changing physiological needs such as feeding and fasting. Disruptions in this zonal architecture are linked to metabolic disorders and liver diseases, underscoring its vital role in preserving liver health and systemic metabolism [2].

Aging induces extensive molecular and structural changes in the liver, disrupting the clear boundaries of hepatic zonation and altering cellular communication. In aged livers, distinct zonal regions become blurred, with the appearance of "bi-zonal" hepatocytes that express markers typically restricted to separate zones. This loss of precise zonal architecture is a defining feature of liver aging and likely contributes to the increased vulnerability to liver dysfunction and metabolic diseases in older adults [3].

When liver zonation is disrupted, the efficiency of metabolic processes declines, causing systemic metabolic imbalances. The failure of specific zones to carry out their specialized functions leads to the buildup of toxins, impaired glucose control, and disrupted lipid metabolism. Structural alterations such as enlarged cells and inflammation further compromise liver function, increasing the risk of developing conditions like fatty liver disease, cirrhosis, and liver cancer, especially in aging populations [4],[5].

Furthermore, the disrupted expression of cadherins, which are critical for cell-cell adhesion, suggests weakened tissue cohesion, facilitating parenchymal remodeling and raising susceptibility to chronic liver conditions. [3]

2.2. Cellular senescence

Hepatic cellular senescence plays a significant role in liver aging, characterized by irreversible cell cycle arrest in liver cells, predominantly hepatocytes, but also including endothelial cells, hepatic stellate cells, and Kupffer cells. As the liver ages, these senescent cells accumulate, leading to functional decline, impaired regenerative capacity, and increased vulnerability to liver diseases [6].

Cellular senescence happens in two main ways. The first, called replicative senescence, happens naturally inside cells when they stop dividing after many rounds of copying themselves. This happens because parts of the chromosome ends, called telomeres, get shorter each time a cell divides. When they get too short, the cell senses this as damage and stops dividing [7].

The second way is premature senescence, which happens when cells are stressed by things like radiation, toxins, or strong oxidative stress. This often involves a different protein called p16, which gradually builds up and blocks the cell's cycle by stopping important proteins (CDK4/6) from working. This keeps a protein called pRB in a form that prevents the cell from entering the phase where it would copy DNA and divide. Senescence caused by p16 is very stable and long-lasting [7]. In simple terms, cells stop dividing because they've been damaged or stressed, and this helps prevent damaged cells from causing problems like cancer.

Both types of senescence show increased levels of tumor suppressor proteins like p53, p16, and p21, as well as the enzyme senescence-associated-β-galactosidase (SA-β-gal), which are commonly used as markers to identify cellular senescence [8].

Senescent cells are metabolically active and release many substances, such as inflammatory molecules like cytokines and chemokines. Together, these make up the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP), which causes ongoing inflammation and changes in tissue structure. The buildup of senescent cells and the resulting chronic inflammation contribute to aging and fibrosis in organs [8].

2.3. Oxidative stress

A major factor driving aging and related diseases is oxidative stress, which occurs when there is a disruption between the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the capacity of antioxidant defense mechanisms to neutralize them. Pathologically elevated levels of reactive oxygen species promote uncontrolled cell growth, programmed cell death, and intensify inflammatory pathways. Chronic, low-grade inflammation, termed "inflammaging," arises from the constant release of pro-inflammatory cytokines by senescent cells, which drives a harmful cycle involving the buildup of ROS, breakdown of the extracellular matrix, apoptosis, and tissue damage. The necrotic cells and disrupted extracellular matrix release signals that further increase inflammation, perpetuating excessive free radical generation and oxidative stress [10].

Aging in the liver is characterized by increased levels of reactive oxygen species, which induce cellular damage by lipid peroxidation, protein modification, and DNA damage. Elevated levels of the Pro-oxidant Antioxidant Balance (PAB) are strongly linked to advanced liver fibrosis stages in patients with MASLD, indicating increased oxidative stress as the disease progresses [11].

Oxidative stress is linked to liver aging through its role in triggering cellular senescence by activating various internal cellular processes. These include causing cell cycle arrest via mediators such as p16 and p21, promoting the production of proinflammatory factors like NF-κB, and persistently activating proteins involved in remodeling the cellular matrix. Additionally, oxidative damage to cell membranes caused by high levels of reactive oxygen and nitrogen species can release danger signals - called damage-associated molecular patterns - that initiate innate inflammatory responses. As shown in animal models, oxidative stress accumulates in the aged liver, which is demonstrated by marked increases in lipofuscin and lipid peroxidation levels, alongside reduced capacity to clear toxic fatty acid species. Together, oxidative stress and aging compromise the liver's capacity to regenerate after injury [12].

2.4 Impaired hepatic autophagy

Autophagy serves as a vital process for recycling cellular components, including the removal of dysfunctional organelles and misfolded proteins [14]. It plays a key role in sustaining liver metabolism and supporting liver regeneration. By supplying hepatocytes with glucose, amino acids, and free fatty acids, autophagy helps maintain their fundamental metabolic activities. Studies show that autophagy  levels are markedly reduced in older livers compared to the young liver [16]. Impaired hepatic autophagy contributes significantly to liver aging by disrupting cellular homeostasis.

Aging in the liver reduces autophagosome formation and function in hepatocytes, leading to lipofuscin accumulation and decreased autophagy enzyme efficacy. Furthermore, lipofuscin accumulation impairs damaged mitochondria turnover, elevating reactive oxygen species. Resultant oxidative stress further disrupts autophagy by exacerbating lysosomal dysfunction. This impairs the clearance of damaged organelles and proteins, promoting steatosis, fibrosis, and reduced regenerative capacity after injury [14].

Additionally, lower levels of autophagy-related proteins like Atg4b and Beclin-1 contribute to mitochondrial dysfunction and heightened susceptibility to ischemia-reperfusion injury in older livers [15].

2.5. Mitochondrial dysfunction

In aging organisms, mitochondrial dysfunction becomes a primary source of reactive oxygen species, largely due to inefficiencies in the electron transport chain [10].  Mitochondria are unique organelles because they have their own DNA called mtDNA﻿, which is crucial for making proteins needed in the energy production process. Research data shows that both the amount of mitochondrial proteins and the activity of the energy-producing system decrease as we age. In aged livers, ATP production declines while free radical generation increases [15]. The mtDNA﻿ is especially vulnerable to damage because it sits close to where ROS are made and lacks protective proteins called histones. This damage causes mutations in the mtDNA﻿. As mitochondria work less efficiently with age, they produce more ROS, and the enzymes that normally remove ROS work less effectively. This creates a harmful cycle where mutations in mtDNA﻿ damage the mitochondria’s energy system, causing even more ROS and oxidative harm. The imbalance between ROS creation and removal leads to damage of proteins and enzymes, speeding up aging and related diseases. Weakened antioxidant defenses in mitochondria increase mutation rates and reduce energy production [13].

This contributes to impaired lipid metabolism, heightened inflammation, fibrosis, and reduced regenerative capacity in the liver. Age-related mitochondrial defects also exacerbate MASLD and alcohol-related liver disease (ARLD). Furthermore, declines in mitochondrial and general hepatic autophagy impair the liver's ability to clear damaged components, worsening disease progression. These mitochondrial alterations, combined with reductions in hepatic volume, blood flow, and metabolizing enzyme function, increase elderly patients’ susceptibility to liver injury and chronic liver disease. Understanding these mechanisms is crucial for developing improved diagnostic and therapeutic strategies tailored for aging populations [12].

3. Liver aging: a pivotal contributor to metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease.

The primary risk factor for chronic liver diseases such as MASLD is advancing age. Collectively, the hallmarks of physiological liver aging described above - loss of hepatic zonation, accumulation of senescent cells, increased oxidative stress, impaired autophagy, and mitochondrial dysfunction - create a metabolic environment that strongly predisposes the liver to MASLD.

The buildup of aged cells in the liver contributes to fat accumulation, which is closely linked to MASLD progression. The detailed processes behind MASLD development are complex and not yet fully understood. A key factor is the excessive accumulation of fat in liver cells, with cellular aging playing a significant role in this fat buildup. This is often explained by the “two-hit” model: the first hit involves an abnormal increase in liver fat due to disrupted fat metabolism, leading to MASLD. The second hit includes liver cell damage caused by oxidative stress, inflammation, and mitochondrial dysfunction, which arises on top of this fat overload. This second hit drives the advancement of MASLD to more severe states such as MASH, fibrosis, cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma [1].

Recently, an improved “multiple-hit” model, complementing the previous model, has become increasingly popular. The improved model demonstrates that interplay among hepatocytes, the gut-liver axis (which is discussed in detail in subsequent sections), and adipose tissue-representing multi-organ crosstalk-plays a pivotal role in MASLD pathogenesis. Furthermore, diverse contributors, such as de novo lipogenesis, lipotoxicity, insulin resistance, hyperuricemia, genetic predispositions, endoplasmic reticulum stress, drive the initiation and progression of MASLD [2], [3].

It is particularly important to emphasize the importance of genetic predisposition and its role in the development of MASLD. Genetic predisposition plays a major role in MASLD pathogenesis. Genome-wide association studies identify some notable genes, including PNPLA3, TM6SF2, GCKR, MBOAT7, and HSD17B13. Notably, PNPLA3 rs738409 emerges as the principal risk variant, fostering hepatic steatosis and accelerating progression of MASD to MASH, fibrosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma, with associated 12-fold heightened HCC risk and 18-fold increased liver-related mortality.​ MBOAT7 variants correlate with accumulation of hepatic lipids and progression of hepatic inflammation [4]. TM6SF2 rs58542926 disrupts very-low-density lipoprotein secretion from hepatocytes, thereby intensifying steatosis severity, while GCKR rs1260326 promotes de novo lipogenesis and intrahepatic fat accumulation [5]. In contrast, HSD17B13 loss-of-function alleles (e.g., rs72613567) confer protection by attenuating lipid droplet formation and mitigating MASLD susceptibility [6].

Patients with MASLD show a higher degree of telomere shortening and greater cell cycle arrest, especially indicated by increased levels of the protein p21. These changes reflect that the liver cells are aging and less capable of regeneration, leading to liver damage and fat buildup. This process plays a significant role in the disease's progression, as the buildup of old, damaged cells promotes inflammation in the liver, making age a major risk factor for MASLD development [7].

In conclusion, advancing age establishes a susceptible hepatic environment that predisposes to MASLD. Age-related hepatic changes in the liver form a foundational layer on which additional metabolic and pathological factors are superimposed. These overlapping factors synergistically exacerbate liver dysfunction and accelerate MASLD progression.

4. Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease and bidirectional connection with gut microbiome

The liver and gut are bidirectionally connected through the portal vein and bile duct (the gut–liver axis). There is growing evidence of specific microbiota profiles in various diseases, including MASLD. In patients with MASLD, these changes commonly include increased abundance of Proteobacteria and Enterobacterales, particularly Gram-negative bacteria such as Escherichia, and reduced levels of beneficial commensal species such as Akkermansia muciniphila and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, which are important producers of anti-inflammatory metabolites like butyrate. Alterations have also been observed in the duodenal mucosal microbiome, where genera such as Serratia and Aggregatibacter appear more frequently in MASLD, suggesting that small-intestinal dysbiosis may play a significant role, especially given its connection to the liver via the bile duct system.

These changes in microbiota composition and function disrupt intestinal barrier integrity, reduce tight‑junction proteins, and damage the mucus layer, increasing intestinal permeability (“leaky gut”). [43]. This disruption allows translocation of microbial products such as pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). PAMPs like LPS and microbial metabolites such as SCFAs, ethanol, and bile acids can move from the gut into the bloodstream and reach the liver through the portal vein. LPS activates Kupffer cells, triggering proinflammatory and fibrogenic responses. Gut dysbiosis disrupts microbial metabolite production, leading to harmful effects on hepatocytes, including inflammation that promotes fibrosis and plays an important role in MASLD pathogenesis by modulating hepatic lipid metabolism, insulin sensitivity, and inflammatory responses [42].

In turn, MASLD progression alters the gut microbiome composition due to changes in bile acid metabolism and impaired bile flow, thereby reducing the delivery of IgA and antimicrobial compounds to the intestine and, as a result, favoring pathogenic overgrowth and perpetuating dysbiosis, creating a self-reinforcing vicious cycle [44]

5.1 Cardiovascular disease

MASLD plays a significant role in the development of cardiovascular disease (CVD). Patients with MASLD have a significantly higher risk of experiencing cardiovascular events such as heart attack, heart failure, or even death from heart-related issues compared to those without the condition. [1]. Nationwide Korean cohorts show MASLD elevates CVD incidence to 8.5 per 1000 person-years versus 6.2 in non-MASLD [2].

The endothelium, which lines blood vessels, normally releases substances that help blood vessels relax, prevent blood clots, and inhibit excessive growth of smooth muscle cells in the vessel walls. In MASLD, this balance is upset, leading to endothelial dysfunction - a key contributor to heart and vessel diseases. A crucial molecule called nitric oxide (NO) helps keep blood vessels open and prevents clotting, but in MASLD, NO production drops due to increased oxidative stress and low-grade inflammation. This reduction is partly caused by higher levels of asymmetric dimethylarginine (ADMA), a natural substance that blocks the enzyme producing NO. Lower NO levels cause blood vessels to narrow, blood pressure to rise, and arteries to stiffen. MASLD also increases endothelin-1 (ET-1), a molecule that tightens blood vessels. While ET-1 normally helps control vessel size, too much ET-1 leads to harmful narrowing and thickening of vessel walls, worsening blood vessel health and accelerating artery disease. Addressing the imbalances in ADMA and ET-1 is vital to improving blood vessel function and reducing cardiovascular risks in MASLD patients [3], [4].

In MASLD, insulin resistance in fat tissue leads to increased breakdown of fats, releasing more free fatty acids into the bloodstream. These fatty acids accumulate in liver cells, raising levels of molecules that interfere with insulin signaling, which further worsens liver insulin resistance. Simultaneously, the liver produces more fat, adding to this build-up. This excess fat stimulates the liver to secrete more very low-density lipoproteins (VLDL), which transport fats through the blood. Higher VLDL levels contribute significantly to the formation of arterial plaques by increasing small, dense LDL particles and lowering protective HDL level. Additionally, liver insulin resistance drives increased insulin levels in the blood by boosting glucose production and reducing insulin clearance, creating a vicious cycle that amplifies inflammation and oxidative stress. These processes promote the development of atherosclerotic plaques - a key event in cardiovascular disease - as VLDL and LDL particles stimulate immune receptors in artery walls, triggering local inflammation [5].

Patients with MASLD - particularly those with liver fibrosis - have a significantly higher risk of atherosclerosis progression due to plaque buildup. While fibrosis shows a stronger association with atherosclerosis progression, MASLD itself remains a significant risk factor [6].

This complex interplay of altered lipid metabolism, insulin resistance, and inflammation in MASLD fuels plaque formation in arteries, heightening cardiovascular risk. Addressing these metabolic imbalances is essential to improve vascular health and reduce heart disease risk in affected individuals.

5.2 Kidney Diseases

Liver metabolic dysfunction has been increasingly recognized as a significant contributor to the development and progression of chronic kidney diseases (CKD) and metabolic dysfunction–associated kidney disease (MDAKD) [1].

One study investigated the link between liver metabolic dysfunction and the development of chronic kidney disease. Researchers performed orthotopic liver transplantation (OLT) from healthy mice to mice with NASH. They compared three groups: untreated NASH mice, NASH mice that underwent a sham surgery and diet change, and NASH mice that received a healthy liver transplant along with a diet change. The mice that received OLT showed a marked decrease in urinary albumin levels and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) compared to the other groups. Additionally, the OLT mice exhibited improvement in kidney structure, including reduced glomerulosclerosis, less tubulointerstitial fibrosis, smaller glomerular volume, and a lower percentage of mesangial area relative to the other groups. These findings demonstrate that liver metabolic dysfunction directly impacts kidney health. [2].

Pathologically increased hepatic lipid accumulation results in elevated production and circulation of free fatty acids and lipoproteins such as very low-density lipoproteins (VLDL), which are absorbed by the kidneys, especially the high-energy-demand tubular cells that rely heavily on fatty acid oxidation. This ectopic lipid deposition induces lipotoxicity in renal cells, triggering oxidative stress and inflammatory responses that foster glomerulosclerosis, tubulointerstitial fibrosis, and progressive renal dysfunction [3].

Activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) also plays an important role in the development of chronic kidney disease (CKD). In MASLD, the liver plays a central role by producing and releasing angiotensinogen into the bloodstream, driven by inflammation and oxidative stress. Angiotensinogen is then converted into angiotensin I by renin, and further transformed into angiotensin II by angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE). Angiotensin II raises blood pressure through systemic vasoconstriction, making it a major risk factor for CKD. Prolonged overactivation of RAAS leads to reduced kidney blood flow, impaired filtration and electrolyte regulation, increased permeability of the kidney’s filtering membrane resulting in protein loss in urine, and eventually causes kidney glomerulosclerosis and fibrosis [4].

This pathophysiological interplay between liver and kidney highlights the liver’s role as a central regulator of systemic metabolic health and underscores the importance of managing hepatic metabolic dysfunction to prevent or slow CKD progression, thereby supporting systemic healthspan preservation.

5.3 Neurodegenerative Diseases

Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease has been increasingly linked to faster brain aging, cognitive decline and neurodegenerative diseases through the emerging concept of the liver-brain axis [1], [5]. Patients with MASLD have shown significantly increased incidence and risk of developing conditions like Alzheimer’s disease, dementia or vascular dementia compared to those without MASLD [2], [3].​

MASLD has been independently linked to increased thickness of the internal carotid artery's intima-media layer, endothelial dysfunction, and greater arterial stiffness. These changes heighten the risk of developing silent cerebrovascular conditions and stroke. Research indicates that individuals with MASLD experience reduced cerebral blood flow and impaired brain perfusion, affecting areas like the grey matter [1]. A study involving over 29,000 participants aged 45 to 82 used MRI brain scans and found that MASLD is linked to reduced volumes of total grey matter and subcortical grey matter. It also showed thinner brain regions typically affected in Alzheimer's disease. This suggests MASLD is associated with brain changes that may contribute to cognitive decline [4].

MASLD promotes faster brain aging by causing ongoing chronic inflammation, low-grade systemic inflammation. Low-grade systemic inflammation can increase the permeability of the blood-brain barrier, allowing harmful substances to enter the brain. This inflammation raises levels of cytokines that can cross the blood-brain barrier, activating brain immune cells such as microglia and astrocytes. This activation leads to neuroinflammation, which disrupts the brain's ability to form new connections, promotes the death of neurons, and speeds up brain aging.  Second, MASLD impairs liver mitochondrial function, leading to excessive production of ROS. This systemic oxidative stress damages neuronal mitochondria, lipids, proteins, and DNA in the brain, further driving neurodegeneration [5].

Insulin resistance, common in MASLD, also negatively impacts brain insulin signaling. This interferes with glucose metabolism in neurons and contributes to Alzheimer’s-related processes, including impaired amyloid-beta clearance and tau protein abnormalities [6].

Together, these mechanisms combine to reduce brain volume, alter brain microstructure, and promote cognitive decline, highlighting MASLD as an important contributor to premature brain aging and neurodegeneration. Managing MASLD and its inflammatory and metabolic factors could help protect brain health.

5.4 Type 2 Diabetes

The relationship between MASLD and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) is bidirectional, with MASLD playing a significant role in the development and progression of T2DM. Evidence indicates that individuals with MASLD have more than twice the risk of developing type 2 diabetes over a five-year period compared to those without MASLD. This underscores the importance of recognizing MASLD not only as a liver condition but also as a critical factor in the development of systemic metabolic diseases such as diabetes [1].

Both MASLD and type 2 diabetes mellitus are diseases that share a close association with insulin resistance. Pathologically insulin resistance often begins in adipose tissue, particularly white adipose tissue (WAT) [2]. Adipocytes possess the capacity for hypertrophic expansion to accommodate substantial lipid storage while maintaining cellular function and homeostasis. However, excessive hypertrophy triggers pro-inflammatory signaling pathways, notably involving the activation of c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) and IκB kinase (IKK), which contribute to the initiation and exacerbation of insulin resistance within these cells [3], [4].  Increased levels of proinflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6 are frequently found in obese individuals with insulin resistance, indicating that inflammation contributes to the condition [4].

Impaired insulin action here leads to defective suppression of lipolysis, resulting in excess release of free fatty acids (FFA) into the circulation, which contributes to insulin resistance in other tissues like muscle and liver [2]. Elevated FFA rather than glucose seems to drive fasting hyperinsulinemia in obese insulin-resistant individuals, as increased fatty acids stimulate insulin secretion [5].

Chronic hyperinsulinemia contributes to hepatic insulin resistance in hepatocytes by reducing insulin receptor levels and impairing Akt phosphorylation. However, the precise relationship between insulin resistance and cellular senescence is not fully understood. To explore this connection in one study, hepatocytes were exposed to high insulin concentrations in vitro. Prolonged insulin treatment caused a significant decrease in insulin receptor expression and nearly abolished insulin responsiveness, as indicated by reduced Akt phosphorylation. At the same time, markers of senescence, including p21 protein and the mRNA levels of p21, p53, and p16, were substantially increased, demonstrating that sustained hyperinsulinemia can induce hepatocyte senescence. To determine whether senescence itself influences insulin resistance, hepatocytes were treated with senescence-inducing agents for five days, and insulin signaling was measured after acute insulin stimulation. Interestingly, senescence did not impair insulin signaling; rather, it enhanced insulin-stimulated phosphorylation. These observations suggest that while chronic high insulin may trigger cellular senescence, senescence appears to improve insulin sensitivity in hepatocytes, acting as a protective mechanism rather than a driver of insulin resistance [6].

One leading mechanism of hepatic insulin resistance involves "selective insulin resistance," where periportal hepatocytes become resistant to insulin’s suppression of gluconeogenesis, while pericentral hepatocytes remain insulin sensitive, sustaining lipogenesis. Periportal hepatocytes mainly mediate gluconeogenesis producing glucose, whereas pericentral hepatocytes mediate glycolysis and fatty acid synthesis, crucial for de novo lipogenesis. This zonal divergence during hyperinsulinemia leads to the paradoxical coexistence of fasting hyperglycemia and hyperlipidemia  [7]. Circulating FFA that may exacerbate insulin resistance more severely in periportal hepatocytes, contributing to the heterogeneous zonal injury seen in metabolic liver diseases [8].

All these factors contribute to the development of MASLD, and in turn, MASLD exacerbates insulin resistance systemically, thereby promoting the progression to T2DM [9].

6. Problem Formulations: Comorbidity Risk Stratification in MASLD

One of the main goals of this work is to address the clinical challenge of stratifying patients with an established diagnosis of MASLD according to their underlying biological heterogeneity and their differential propensity for extrahepatic disease development over time. MASLD represents an increasingly prevalent condition, the risk of which rises with advancing age, and it affects a substantial proportion of the adult population. As a chronic metabolic disorder, its progression and associated systemic manifestations can significantly impair healthspan. At the same time, considerable interindividual variability in disease expression and progression is observed among patients with MASLD, underscoring the need for systematic phenotypic characterization that can refine risk stratification and delineate patient profiles with distinct disease trajectories. Such an approach may improve the prediction of long-term comorbidity burden and support more personalized monitoring and preventive strategies. Taking into account differences in data availability and analytical objectives, three complementary analytical formulations are proposed, ranging from fully supervised to unsupervised approaches. Together, these strategies aim to enable data-driven phenotypic stratification of MASLD  and to support precision-oriented risk assessment and management.

6.1. Supervised longitudinal (temporal) comorbidity risk classification

When longitudinal outcome labels are available - specifically when datasets indicate which patients with MASLD later develop particular comorbid conditions - the phenotyping task can be formulated as a multi-label prediction problem:

f(x) → (y₁, y₂, …, yK),

where x ∈ ℝⁿ denotes the patient’s baseline feature vector composed of clinical and laboratory measurements organized into clinically meaningful domains (e.g., MENTION SOME OF Liver, Lipid, Glucose, Thyroid, Stiffness, Inflammation, BMI, Kidney, Ultrasound, CHD anamnesis), and each yₖ ∈ {0, 1} represents the presence or absence of comorbidity k during follow-up. Each outcome yₖ corresponds to a clinically relevant comorbidity or disease phenotype — such as cardiovascular, diabetic, thyroid-related, or nephrologic complications — that may develop over time in the context of MASLD.

A multi-label formulation is preferred over a single-label approach because patients with MASLD may develop multiple comorbid conditions, either simultaneously or sequentially, reflecting shared and overlapping metabolic and pathophysiological mechanisms. For instance, a single patient may present with both cardiovascular and diabetic complications, or with a combination of organ-specific and inflammatory phenotypes. Furthermore, clinical investigation data can frequently be organized into clinically meaningful domains. For example, ABL, ZBL, total cholesterol, and triglycerides reflect lipid metabolism, whereas insulin, HbA1c, HOMA, and C-peptide capture glucose tolerance and insulin resistance. This inherent domain structure can be leveraged during modeling through feature grouping or domain-aware regularization.

6.2. Survival analysis with competing risks

Since outcome data are longitudinal, a more informative formulation models the time to comorbidity onset rather than binary occurrence. When a patient may develop multiple comorbidities over time, with development of one potentially altering the risk of developing others, the problem is formulated as competing risks survival analysis:

h_k(t | x) — cause-specific hazard of comorbidity k at time t given baseline biomarkers x

This formulation answers both "whether" and "when" a patient is likely to develop each comorbidity, and correctly accounts for the competing nature of outcomes. This longitudinal framing naturally extends the Markov state-transition perspective: MASLD represents the entry state, and transitions to specific comorbid states are modeled by cause-specific hazard functions, with biomarker profiles governing transition rates.

6.3. Unsupervised phenotyping for cohort discovery

In settings where outcome labels are absent, or as a complementary exploratory step prior to 
supervised analysis, an unsupervised approach is applied to discover natural patient subgroups 
within the MASLD cohort based on their biomarker profiles:

cluster(x) → phenotype_k

Clustering algorithms partition MASLD patients into subgroups with similar laboratory patterns. 
Once clusters are identified, each cluster is characterized by examining which biomarker domains 
dominate its centroid (e.g., a cluster with elevated LDL-C and triglycerides is labeled a 
cardiovascular-risk phenotype). 
This hypothesis-free discovery approach may reveal phenotypes not anticipated by prior clinical knowledge, 
and is particularly valuable for exploratory cohorts. In addition, unsupervised phenotyping is more robust 
to confounders such as alcohol use and obesity, which distort disease results.
