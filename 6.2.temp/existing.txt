Tree-based ensemble methods, such as random forests, gradient boosting machines (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost), and their variants, have demonstrated particular effectiveness in clinical prediction tasks. These models capture non-linear feature interactions, handle missing data natively, tolerate mixed data types, and provide measures of feature importance (e.g., via SHAP values), which supports clinical interpretation of model predictions. In MASLD research, explainable gradient boosting models have been shown to outperform conventional non-invasive scoring systems such as FIB-4 and the NAFLD Fibrosis Score in screening for advanced fibrosis[1], illustrating the clinical utility of supervised ML approaches.
Deep learning architectures, including feedforward neural networks and recurrent models, extend supervised learning to high-dimensional and sequential data but typically require substantially larger training sets and are less interpretable without the use of post-hoc explanation techniques.

Survival analysis as a supervised framework for longitudinal data
A particularly important specialized extension of supervised learning for MASLD research is survival analysis—also referred to as time-to-event modeling. Although survival analysis is sometimes treated as a separate statistical discipline, it is fundamentally a supervised learning problem: the model learns from labeled training data in which each observation is associated with a known or censored outcome and a time variable. What distinguishes survival analysis from standard classification or regression is its explicit handling of censored observations (patients who have not yet experienced the event by the end of the follow-up period) and its modeling of when, not merely whether, an outcome occurs. This temporal dimension is critical for clinical decision-making, as the timing of comorbidity onset directly influences monitoring intervals, therapeutic urgency, and preventive strategies.
The classical Cox proportional hazards model - the most widely used survival framework - estimates how covariates shift the baseline hazard but relies on the proportional hazards assumption and cannot capture non-linear effects. Machine learning extensions such as random survival forests, gradient-boosted survival models, and deep learning approaches (e.g., DeepSurv) relax these constraints while preserving the supervised structure of learning from censored time-to-event data.
In MASLD, patients may develop multiple comorbid conditions over time, and the occurrence of one (e.g., cardiovascular disease) may alter the risk of another (e.g., chronic kidney disease). This gives rise to a competing risk formulation, addressed by methods such as the Fine-Gray model, competing-risks random survival forests, and deep learning architectures like DeepHit and its dynamic extension, which handle longitudinal measurements evolving over time - a property highly relevant for MASLD. This longitudinal, multi-outcome framing connects naturally to a Markov state-transition perspective: MASLD represents the entry state, transitions to specific comorbid states are governed by cause-specific hazard functions, and time-varying biomarker profiles modulate transition rates—answering both whether and when a patient is likely to develop each comorbidity.
6.1.2. Unsupervised learning
Unsupervised learning algorithms discover inherent structure in data without relying on predefined outcome labels. In the MASLD context, this paradigm is especially valuable for hypothesis-free phenotype discovery - identifying natural patient subgroups that share similar biomarker profiles, which may correspond to distinct disease mechanisms and risk trajectories not anticipated by prior clinical knowledge.
Clustering algorithms are the primary unsupervised tool for phenotypic stratification. Common clustering methods include K-means, which partitions patients into k groups by minimizing within-cluster variance but assumes spherical cluster geometry; Gaussian mixture models, which relax this assumption by modeling clusters as multivariate Gaussian distributions with probabilistic assignments - particularly useful when phenotype boundaries are not sharp; hierarchical clustering, which builds nested dendrograms allowing post-hoc selection of cluster granularity; and spectral clustering, which operates on patient similarity graphs to detect non-convex structures. Each method embodies distinct assumptions about cluster shape, number, and assignment certainty, and the choice among them should be guided by the expected geometry and overlap of clinical subgroups in the data.
Before clustering, dimensionality reduction is often applied to high-dimensional biomarker data. Principal component analysis (PCA) identifies linear axes of maximum variance, reducing the feature space while preserving the dominant directions of variation. Non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) decomposes the data matrix into additive, non-negative components that are often more biologically interpretable - for example, identifying latent processes such as metabolic decline, inflammatory activation, or fibrotic remodeling. For visualization, techniques such as t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) and uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) project high-dimensional data into two or three dimensions while preserving local neighborhood structure, facilitating visual inspection of cluster separation. Autoencoders - neural networks trained to reconstruct their input through a low-dimensional bottleneck - can capture non-linear latent structure that linear methods may miss.
In MASLD research, unsupervised approaches have already demonstrated utility. Studies employing K-means clustering, non-negative matrix factorization, and data-driven clustering methods have identified distinct MASLD subtypes—ranging from lean metabolically mild phenotypes to inflammatory, hepatotoxic, and cardiometabolic subtypes - with differential risks of liver-related and extrahepatic complications. The unsupervised nature of these methods is particularly advantageous for MASLD phenotyping because it avoids reliance on potentially biased or incomplete outcome labels and is more robust to confounders such as alcohol use and obesity, which can distort disease-specific outcome associations.
Once clusters are identified, they are characterized post hoc by examining the biomarker domains that dominate each cluster centroid. For example, a cluster with elevated LDL cholesterol, triglycerides, and markers of insulin resistance may be interpreted as a cardiovascular-risk phenotype, while a cluster dominated by elevated transaminases and liver stiffness may represent a hepatotoxic phenotype. External validation using clinical outcomes—such as rates of cardiovascular events, diabetes incidence, or liver-related mortality—provides evidence that the discovered phenotypes are clinically meaningful.
6.1.3. Reinforcement learning
Reinforcement learning (RL) trains an agent to make sequential decisions by interacting with an environment and receiving feedback in the form of rewards or penalties. The agent learns a policy - a mapping from states (e.g., a patient's current clinical condition) to actions (e.g., treatment decisions) - that maximizes cumulative long-term reward. This makes RL particularly suited to dynamic treatment regime optimization, where the goal is to determine the best sequence of interventions over time, accounting for how a patient's state evolves in response to treatment. MASLD aligns well with RL formulations: it is a chronic, progressive condition in which treatment decisions - lifestyle interventions, pharmacotherapy initiation, dose adjustments, monitoring frequency - must be made repeatedly as the patient's metabolic and hepatic status changes, and the optimal strategy may differ substantially across individuals.
Methodologically, clinical RL draws on value-based methods (e.g., Q-learning, deep Q-networks), which estimate expected long-term reward for each state–action pair, and policy-based methods (e.g., policy gradient algorithms), which directly optimize the treatment policy. Offline (batch) RL is especially relevant, as it learns optimal policies from existing observational data without requiring real-time patient interaction. In liver disease research, RL has been applied to treatment policy evaluation for hepatitis C virus, optimizing the timing and selection of direct-acting antiviral therapy; in the broader chronic disease context, it has been explored for insulin dosing in diabetes, chemotherapy scheduling in oncology, and intervention planning in critical care.
While RL has not yet been widely applied to MASLD management specifically, its potential grows as pharmacological therapies—including GLP-1 receptor agonists and other emerging agents—expand the clinical decision space and increase the need for personalized, adaptive treatment strategies. However, translation to clinical deployment faces substantial challenges in interpretability, safety validation, reward signal definition, and regulatory acceptance.
6.2. Paradigm selection for MASLD phenotyping and risk stratification
The choice among ML paradigms is determined by the research objective and data characteristics. Unsupervised learning is best suited for exploratory phenotype discovery in MASLD cohorts where outcome labels are absent or unreliable, enabling hypothesis-free identification of patient subgroups with distinct metabolic profiles. Supervised learning, including its survival analysis and semi-supervised extensions, is appropriate when labeled longitudinal outcomes are available (or partially available), enabling predictive risk stratification, time-to-event modeling for specific comorbidities, and improved generalization under label scarcity. Reinforcement learning addresses the complementary problem of dynamic treatment optimization, determining not just who is at risk but what to do about it over time. In practice, these paradigms are not mutually exclusive. An effective analytical pipeline for MASLD phenotyping may combine unsupervised clustering to discover phenotypes, supervised or survival models to predict comorbidity risk within each phenotype, and semi-supervised methods to improve model performance when labels are scarce.
